The 1905 General Strike: Finnish Workers and Political Reforms

🇬🇧 In English
The 1905 General Strike was a pivotal moment in Finnish history, marking the first time that workers and ordinary citizens united to demand political change. Sparked by growing discontent with Russian rule, economic hardship, and a desire for greater democratic rights, the strike mobilized thousands of Finns and led to significant political reforms. For those studying for the Finnish citizenship test, understanding the 1905 General Strike provides insight into the Finnish people’s role in shaping their political landscape and their commitment to social justice and democratic values.

Background: Political and Economic Conditions in Finland

By the early 20th century, Finland, an autonomous Grand Duchy under the Russian Empire, was experiencing increasing political repression and economic difficulties. Russia had implemented a series of “Russification” policies aimed at integrating Finland more closely into the empire, which restricted Finnish autonomy and threatened Finnish culture. These measures included attempts to limit the Finnish language, reduce the powers of Finnish institutions, and impose Russian law on the Finnish people. The economic situation was also challenging, with rising inflation and difficult working conditions, particularly in industrial cities. Finnish workers faced low wages, long hours, and limited labor rights. Discontent with these conditions was widespread, and calls for political and social reform grew louder as the working class became increasingly organized.

Causes of the 1905 General Strike

The 1905 General Strike was influenced by several factors, including:
  1. Russification Policies: The Russian Empire’s efforts to limit Finland’s autonomy fueled resentment among Finns from all social classes. Many saw Russification as a direct threat to Finnish identity, language, and culture, and opposition to these policies united different segments of society.
  2. Economic Hardship and Labor Conditions: The working class faced poor labor conditions, including low wages, long hours, and unsafe workplaces. Workers sought fair treatment, better wages, and improved working conditions, and labor unions began organizing to advocate for these rights.
  3. The Russian Revolution of 1905: In 1905, a wave of protests and strikes spread across the Russian Empire, following violent repression of a peaceful workers’ demonstration in St. Petersburg, known as Bloody Sunday. The events in Russia inspired similar protests in Finland, where workers and activists saw an opportunity to challenge Russian rule and demand political reforms.

The General Strike: Unity and Demand for Reforms

The Finnish General Strike began on October 29, 1905, and quickly spread across the country. Factories, railways, ports, and public services shut down as thousands of workers and citizens joined the movement, bringing the economy to a halt. The strike was unique in its widespread participation, as people from all walks of life—urban and rural, laborers and intellectuals—joined together to push for change.
  1. Unified Demands: The strikers presented a list of demands calling for political and social reforms. These included the establishment of a Finnish parliament elected by universal suffrage, the restoration of Finnish autonomy, freedom of assembly and press, and improved working conditions. The unity of workers, intellectuals, and other citizens around these demands demonstrated the collective desire for greater democratic rights and an end to Russification.
  2. Role of Workers and Unions: Finnish workers and labor unions played a leading role in organizing the strike, which was unprecedented in its scale and impact. Trade unions, political groups, and social organizations came together to coordinate efforts, showcasing the growing influence of organized labor in Finland. The strike empowered Finnish workers, giving them a voice in political matters and reinforcing the idea that ordinary citizens could drive change.
  3. Widespread Support and Peaceful Protest: Although the strike caused disruptions across the country, it was largely peaceful, with minimal violence. Demonstrators held rallies, marches, and meetings to express their demands, showing solidarity and discipline. The peaceful nature of the strike helped gain support from a broad spectrum of society and reinforced the legitimacy of the movement’s goals.

Political Reforms and the Resulting Achievements

The success of the 1905 General Strike led to immediate political reforms that fundamentally changed Finland’s political landscape.
  1. Establishment of the Finnish Parliament (Eduskunta): In response to the demands of the strikers, Tsar Nicholas II agreed to establish a new Finnish parliament, the Eduskunta, which replaced the Finnish Diet. The Eduskunta was to be elected by universal suffrage, making Finland one of the first countries in the world to grant all adult citizens, including women, the right to vote and stand for election. This was a groundbreaking achievement in the history of democracy, setting Finland apart as a pioneer of equal political rights.
  2. Restoration of Finnish Autonomy: The strike forced Russian authorities to halt their Russification policies temporarily, restoring Finnish autonomy and protecting Finnish language, culture, and institutions. Although later efforts to impose Russification would reemerge, the success of the 1905 General Strike was a significant victory for Finnish self-determination.
  3. Labor Reforms and Improved Workers’ Rights: In the wake of the strike, Finland implemented labor reforms that improved working conditions, including limitations on working hours and measures to address wage disparities. The success of the strike gave the labor movement momentum, leading to a stronger role for trade unions and increased awareness of workers’ rights in Finnish society.
  4. Freedom of Press and Assembly: The success of the 1905 General Strike also resulted in the recognition of basic civil rights, including freedom of press, assembly, and association. These freedoms allowed Finnish political organizations, social movements, and newspapers to operate openly, fostering a culture of political engagement and civic responsibility.

Long-lasting Impact of the 1905 General Strike on Finnish Society

The 1905 General Strike left a lasting impact on Finnish society, setting a precedent for organized protest and paving the way for future political and social reforms.
  1. A Foundation for Finnish Democracy: The establishment of the Eduskunta and the introduction of universal suffrage provided a foundation for Finnish democracy. The 1905 General Strike demonstrated that the Finnish people were willing and able to advocate for democratic principles, creating a legacy of civic participation that remains a defining feature of Finnish political life.
  2. Strengthening of the Labor Movement: The strike empowered the labor movement in Finland, giving workers a voice in politics and encouraging the growth of trade unions. The increased influence of organized labor would continue to shape Finland’s labor laws and social policies, ultimately leading to the development of a strong welfare state.
  3. Rise of National Unity and Identity: The 1905 General Strike united Finns from different backgrounds and social classes in a common cause, strengthening national unity and pride. The success of the strike against Russian oppression reinforced Finland’s commitment to self-determination and laid the groundwork for the eventual push for independence in 1917.
  4. Legacy of Peaceful Protest: The peaceful nature of the 1905 General Strike set a precedent for nonviolent resistance in Finland, underscoring the effectiveness of organized, peaceful protest. This tradition of peaceful civic engagement has continued to shape Finnish activism, encouraging dialogue and cooperation in pursuit of social and political change.

Conclusion: The 1905 General Strike’s Role in Finnish Independence and Democracy

The 1905 General Strike was a turning point in Finnish history, showing the power of collective action and the strength of Finnish society in the face of adversity. The strike’s success in achieving political reforms and expanding democratic rights remains a source of pride for Finland, symbolizing the Finnish commitment to social justice, equality, and self-determination. For those studying for the Finnish citizenship test, the 1905 General Strike exemplifies the resilience of the Finnish people and their role in shaping a democratic and inclusive society. The legacy of the strike continues to influence Finland’s political landscape, reminding future generations of the importance of civic engagement and peaceful activism.
