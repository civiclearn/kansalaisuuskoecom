The Language Struggle: Finnish vs. Swedish in the 19th Century

🇬🇧 In English
The 19th-century language struggle between Finnish and Swedish was a defining chapter in Finland’s history, shaping the nation’s cultural identity and laying the foundation for linguistic equality. For centuries, Swedish had been the language of the elite, government, and education, while Finnish was primarily spoken by the rural majority. However, as the Finnish national awakening gained momentum, Finnish intellectuals, artists, and reformers began advocating for the elevation of Finnish to an official status equal to Swedish. For those studying for the Finnish citizenship test, understanding the language struggle offers insight into Finland’s journey toward cultural independence, social equity, and national unity.

Historical Background: Finland’s Linguistic Landscape

Swedish influence in Finland began with the establishment of Swedish rule in the 12th and 13th centuries. For centuries, Swedish served as the dominant language of administration, education, and the legal system, while Finnish remained primarily a spoken language among the common people. By the 19th century, however, as Finland was under Russian rule, a growing movement sought to elevate Finnish and establish it as a language of culture, governance, and literature. During this period, the majority of Finland’s population spoke Finnish as their first language. However, Swedish maintained a privileged position, and Swedish-speaking elites dominated politics, business, and academia. This linguistic divide reinforced social and class distinctions, with Swedish representing status and authority, while Finnish symbolized the everyday lives of the common people.

The Rise of Finnish National Consciousness

As Finland’s national awakening took root in the 19th century, Finnish intellectuals and cultural leaders began to advocate for the recognition of Finnish as a literary and administrative language. This movement, often referred to as “Fennomania,” was driven by a desire to affirm Finland’s cultural identity and assert its distinctiveness within the Russian Empire.
  1. The Role of Literature and the Kalevala: Literature played a key role in promoting the Finnish language. The publication of the Kalevala, compiled by Elias Lönnrot, brought Finland’s oral traditions to a wider audience and inspired pride in Finnish culture. The Kalevala became a symbol of the Finnish national awakening and demonstrated the potential of Finnish as a language of literature.
  2. Fennoman and Svecoman Movements: The language struggle led to the emergence of two distinct political and cultural groups: the Fennomans, who advocated for the elevation of Finnish, and the Svecomans, who sought to preserve Swedish as the primary language of education and administration. The Fennoman movement, led by influential figures like Johan Vilhelm Snellman, viewed the elevation of Finnish as essential to strengthening national identity, while the Svecomans argued that Swedish was an integral part of Finland’s heritage and should remain the dominant language.
  3. Promotion of Finnish in Education: Finnish intellectuals emphasized the need for education in Finnish to reach a broader population. As Finnish literature, newspapers, and schools grew in prominence, more Finns became literate in their native language, fostering a sense of unity and cultural pride. The push for Finnish-language education became a crucial part of the language struggle, as schools played a significant role in shaping Finland’s national identity.

Legislative and Political Progress: The Push for Official Bilingualism

The language struggle gained political traction in the mid-19th century, as Finnish advocates pushed for legislative recognition of Finnish as an official language.
  1. The Finnish Diet and Language Rights: The Finnish Diet, reconvened in 1863, provided a platform for the language debate. With the Diet as a legislative body, Finnish representatives could advocate for policies that promoted the use of Finnish in governance, education, and public life. The Diet’s discussions on language rights underscored the growing demand for linguistic equality and highlighted the deep-seated divisions between the Fennoman and Svecoman factions.
  2. Alexander II’s Language Decree of 1863: Tsar Alexander II issued a language decree in 1863, marking a significant step toward linguistic equality. The decree granted Finnish equal status alongside Swedish in government and administration, though it allowed a 20-year transition period. This decree symbolized official recognition of Finnish and encouraged the gradual adoption of Finnish in legal and administrative matters.
  3. The School Act of 1866: The School Act introduced reforms that supported the use of Finnish in education, leading to an increase in Finnish-language schools. Finnish gradually replaced Swedish as the primary language of instruction, especially in rural areas where Finnish-speaking populations were predominant. This shift laid the groundwork for a more inclusive educational system that reflected Finland’s linguistic diversity.

Cultural Impacts of the Language Struggle

The language struggle had profound cultural implications, as it not only elevated Finnish but also reshaped Finnish literature, arts, and public life.
  1. Finnish Literature and Journalism: The rise of Finnish-language literature, newspapers, and journals helped disseminate the ideas of the national awakening and connected the Finnish-speaking population with the emerging political discourse. Publications like Suometar, a Finnish-language newspaper, spread Fennoman ideas and promoted Finnish cultural pride. Finnish authors began writing novels, poems, and essays that celebrated Finnish identity, contributing to a growing body of national literature.
  2. Artistic Representation of Finnish Identity: Visual arts also played a role in the language struggle, as artists sought to portray Finland’s landscapes, people, and folklore. Figures like Akseli Gallen-Kallela, whose work often depicted scenes from the Kalevala, reinforced the connection between Finnish art and cultural heritage. The arts helped define Finland’s unique cultural identity, inspiring national pride and fostering unity among Finnish speakers.
  3. Emphasis on Bilingualism: While the Fennoman movement advocated for Finnish as a primary language, the eventual outcome was a system of bilingualism that recognized the value of both languages. The preservation of Swedish as a second official language alongside Finnish reflected Finland’s historical ties to Sweden, acknowledging the linguistic diversity within the country and promoting mutual respect between linguistic communities.

Lasting Effects and Legacy of the Language Struggle

The language struggle of the 19th century had a lasting impact on Finland, establishing the foundation for a bilingual society and fostering a sense of national unity that transcended linguistic differences.
  1. Official Bilingualism in Finland: The language struggle ultimately led to the recognition of Finnish and Swedish as equal official languages. This system of bilingualism, enshrined in Finnish law, allows citizens to access government services, education, and legal proceedings in both languages. Bilingualism has become a defining feature of Finnish society, emphasizing inclusivity and respect for linguistic diversity.
  2. Strengthened Finnish Identity: The successful elevation of Finnish reinforced the country’s sense of national identity. As Finnish became the language of literature, education, and governance, it united the population and provided a cultural foundation that supported Finland’s eventual independence in 1917. The language struggle was instrumental in fostering a collective Finnish identity, encouraging pride in the language and culture.
  3. Legacy in Education and Culture: The language struggle inspired generations of Finns to appreciate and preserve their linguistic heritage. Today, the legacy of the language movement is celebrated through Finnish literature, music, and cultural festivals. Finnish and Swedish are both valued as integral parts of the nation’s heritage, and bilingualism is widely embraced as a source of strength and cultural richness.

Conclusion: The Language Struggle’s Role in Finnish National Identity

The language struggle between Finnish and Swedish in the 19th century was a defining period that shaped Finland’s national identity, unity, and cultural pride. Through the efforts of intellectuals, politicians, and educators, Finnish was elevated to an equal status with Swedish, empowering Finnish speakers and fostering a spirit of inclusivity. The language struggle reflects Finland’s commitment to linguistic diversity and its enduring values of equality and mutual respect. For those studying for the Finnish citizenship test, understanding the language struggle highlights Finland’s journey toward social equality and the importance of preserving its linguistic heritage. The bilingual legacy of the language struggle continues to define Finnish society, reminding future generations of the strength that comes from unity and respect for cultural differences.
