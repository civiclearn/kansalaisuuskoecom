Finnish National Awakening: Elias Lönnrot and the Kalevala (1835)

🇬🇧 In English
The Finnish National Awakening in the 19th century marked a period of cultural and national self-discovery, as Finland, under Russian rule, sought to affirm its unique identity and heritage. Central to this movement was Elias Lönnrot’s Kalevala, an epic collection of Finnish folk tales, poems, and myths first published in 1835. This work became the foundation of Finnish literature and a symbol of national pride, inspiring Finnish society to preserve and celebrate its culture. For those studying for the Finnish citizenship test, understanding the role of the Kalevala and the contributions of Elias Lönnrot offers insight into Finland’s journey toward cultural and political independence.

Historical Context: Finland under Russian Rule

Finland was under Swedish control for centuries until the Treaty of Fredrikshamn in 1809, which transferred it to the Russian Empire as an autonomous Grand Duchy. While Finnish society retained some self-governance and cultural freedoms, the 19th century brought a desire for greater cultural independence and a distinct Finnish identity separate from both Swedish and Russian influences. The early 1800s saw the rise of romantic nationalism across Europe, a movement emphasizing pride in one’s language, history, and folklore. Inspired by these ideas, Finnish intellectuals, artists, and scholars embarked on a mission to elevate Finnish language and culture, laying the groundwork for the Finnish National Awakening. A key figure in this movement was Elias Lönnrot, a physician, folklorist, and linguist dedicated to preserving Finland’s folk traditions.

Elias Lönnrot’s Journey and the Creation of the Kalevala

Elias Lönnrot was born in 1802 in Sammatti, a small village in Finland. As a young man, he developed a passion for the Finnish language and folklore. Lönnrot believed that collecting and preserving traditional Finnish stories, songs, and poetry could foster a sense of unity and pride among Finns. Starting in the 1820s, he began traveling through rural areas in Karelia and other Finnish regions, recording oral poetry and folk songs recited by local storytellers. Lönnrot’s expeditions, which spanned more than a decade, allowed him to gather a vast collection of stories, myths, and ancient songs known as runo poetry. Inspired by epic poems like Homer’s Iliad and Odyssey, Lönnrot organized and compiled the tales into a single narrative structure, creating what would become Finland’s national epic, the Kalevala. The Kalevala, first published in 1835 and later expanded in 1849, brought together the stories of mythical heroes, gods, and creatures, capturing the essence of Finnish folklore. The epic follows characters such as Väinämöinen, a wise shaman and singer; Ilmarinen, a blacksmith; and Lemminkäinen, a warrior, as they embark on quests, confront enemies, and seek to control the magical artifact called the Sampo. These characters and their adventures reflected the values, challenges, and triumphs of the Finnish people.

The Kalevala as a Catalyst for the Finnish National Awakening

The publication of the Kalevala had a profound impact on Finnish society. For the first time, Finns had a written work that celebrated their culture, language, and identity. The Kalevala helped fuel the Finnish National Awakening, inspiring pride in Finnish heritage and uniting people around a shared sense of identity.
  1. Cultural Identity and Language: The Kalevala emphasized the beauty and richness of the Finnish language, which had historically been overshadowed by Swedish, the language of administration and the elite. Lönnrot’s work helped elevate Finnish to a literary language, sparking a linguistic movement that would ultimately lead to Finnish gaining official status alongside Swedish.
  2. National Pride and Unity: The characters and stories of the Kalevala embodied the values of resilience, courage, and ingenuity. As Finns read about mythical heroes overcoming challenges, the Kalevala became a symbol of national pride and unity. It reinforced the idea that Finns had a distinct and valuable cultural heritage worthy of preservation.
  3. Inspiration for Finnish Arts and Literature: The Kalevala inspired a generation of Finnish artists, writers, and composers. Figures like the painter Akseli Gallen-Kallela and the composer Jean Sibelius drew heavily from the Kalevala in their work, helping to establish a unique Finnish cultural identity in the arts. Gallen-Kallela’s paintings, which depict scenes and characters from the epic, remain iconic representations of Finnish mythology.
  4. Influence on Education and Civic Life: The Kalevala became part of Finland’s educational system, with schools teaching the epic to promote cultural literacy and pride. This incorporation of the Kalevala into education helped generations of Finns connect with their heritage, creating a stronger foundation for civic life and national unity.

Long-lasting Effects of the Kalevala on Finnish Society

The Kalevala has left a lasting legacy in Finnish society, shaping the country’s cultural and national identity in profound ways.
  1. Development of Finnish Literature: The Kalevala marked the beginning of a flourishing Finnish literary tradition. It inspired writers to create works in the Finnish language, and it set a precedent for using literature as a means of exploring and celebrating national identity. The Finnish literary tradition, which began with the Kalevala, eventually produced other influential works and continues to thrive today.
  2. Cultural Symbol in Finland’s Independence Movement: The Kalevala became a rallying point for Finland’s independence movement in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. As Finnish national consciousness grew, the Kalevala was a reminder of Finland’s unique heritage and history. This sense of shared identity contributed to the Finnish push for autonomy and eventual independence from Russia in 1917.
  3. International Recognition and Cultural Exchange: The Kalevala brought Finnish folklore to the attention of the world, sparking international interest in Finnish culture and mythology. Translations of the epic have allowed readers around the globe to experience Finnish mythology, fostering cultural exchange and highlighting Finland’s contributions to world literature.
  4. Modern Influence and Popular Culture: Today, the Kalevala continues to inspire Finnish culture and popular media, from literature and visual arts to films and music. Elements of the epic can be found in modern adaptations, video games, and fantasy literature, ensuring that the stories of the Kalevala remain relevant and accessible to contemporary audiences. The Kalevala is celebrated each year on Kalevala Day, February 28, which recognizes its significance in Finnish culture.

The Role of the Kalevala in Finnish National Identity

The Kalevala has become more than an epic poem; it is a foundational text for Finnish identity. It encapsulates the values of sisu—perseverance, resilience, and resourcefulness—that are central to Finnish character. For many Finns, the Kalevala symbolizes the strength and ingenuity of their ancestors, providing a connection to Finland’s ancient past. For those studying for the Finnish citizenship test, understanding the Kalevala and its impact on Finnish national identity offers valuable insight into Finland’s journey toward independence, self-expression, and cultural pride. The work of Elias Lönnrot and the stories within the Kalevala continue to resonate, serving as reminders of the power of heritage, community, and the enduring spirit of Finland.
